Clinical Assessment:

Based on Mr. Smith's past medical history and the history of present illness, the top clinical differentials to consider for his chest pain would include:

1. Acute Coronary Syndrome (Unstable Angina or Myocardial Infarction) - Given his risk factors for coronary artery disease and the characteristic chest pain radiating to the arms and jaw, ACS is the most concerning and likely diagnosis.
2. Coronary Artery Disease (Stable Angina) - Chronic stable angina may present similarly to ACS but with a history of predictable triggers for chest pain, such as exertion.
3. Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) - Chest pain due to reflux can sometimes mimic cardiac chest pain, especially in the absence of other typical symptoms like regurgitation.
4. Musculoskeletal Chest Pain - Strain or inflammation of the chest wall muscles or cartilage could cause chest pain exacerbated by certain movements.
5. Anxiety or Panic Attack - Psychological stressors can present with symptoms similar to cardiac chest pain, although less likely in the context of Mr. Smith's risk factors.
